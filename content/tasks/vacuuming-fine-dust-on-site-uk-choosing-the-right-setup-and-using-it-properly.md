## The problem

Vacuuming fine construction dust is often treated as a basic cleanup task. In reality, it’s one of the most common ways dust gets put back into the air. The wrong vacuum, blocked filters, or poor technique can turn “cleaning” into another dust-generating activity.

Fine dust from concrete, plaster, and masonry settles everywhere. If it’s picked up with unsuitable equipment, it passes straight through the vacuum and back into the room.

Common ways vacuuming goes wrong:

- Using domestic or non-rated vacuums.
- Running vacuums with clogged or damaged filters.
- Emptying vacuums in open spaces where dust escapes.

The result is dust being re-suspended during cleanup, often undoing the controls used during the task itself.

## What “good” looks like on site vacuuming

Good dust vacuuming is about containment and filtration:

- The vacuum is rated for fine construction dust.
- Filters are intact and maintained.
- Dust is captured and retained, not blown back into the space.

When vacuuming is done properly, cleanup reduces airborne dust rather than stirring it back up.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when vacuuming fine dust:

- **M-class (or better) vacuums**  
  Use vacuums designed for hazardous fine dust, with proper filtration and sealing.

- **Filter maintenance**  
  Check and clean filters regularly. Blocked filters reduce airflow and increase dust leakage.

- **Bagged systems**  
  Use sealed dust bags or liners to contain fine dust during emptying.

- **Controlled emptying**  
  Empty vacuums in designated areas, away from occupied or clean spaces.

- **Slow, methodical passes**  
  Vacuum slowly to allow fine dust to be captured rather than scattered.

## What to avoid

- **Using domestic vacuums**  
  They leak fine dust and re-release it into the room.

- **Emptying vacuums in finished spaces**  
  This releases dust back into the environment you’ve just cleaned.

- **Banging filters to “clean” them indoors**  
  This launches fine dust back into the air.

- **Rushing cleanup**  
  Fast passes over dusty surfaces tend to stir dust rather than capture it.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection can be useful during vacuuming in heavily contaminated spaces. It doesn’t compensate for poor filtration or leaking equipment.

If vacuuming visibly releases dust into the air, the equipment or method is not adequate.

## A simple baseline setup

For most site cleanup involving fine dust, a workable baseline looks like this:

- M-class vacuum with intact filters
- Sealed dust bags or liners
- Emptying done away from clean areas
- Slow, methodical vacuuming passes
- Final wipe-down of surfaces after vacuuming

This setup ensures vacuuming actually removes dust instead of redistributing it.

## UK context

In the UK, equipment used to control and remove hazardous dust is expected to be suitable for the task. Using vacuums that are not rated for fine construction dust is unlikely to be considered reasonable control where hazardous dust is present.

If dust is visibly escaping during vacuuming, the equipment and method are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- M-class vacuum used  
- Filters intact and clean  
- Sealed bags or liners fitted  
- Emptying done away from clean spaces  
- Slow, careful vacuuming passes  

Ticking these five keeps cleanup from becoming another dust exposure event.
