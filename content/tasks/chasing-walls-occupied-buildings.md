## The problem

Chasing walls indoors creates a dense cloud of fine dust. In occupied buildings, the issue is not just exposure for the person holding the tool. Dust travels into corridors, adjacent rooms, and shared spaces, where people who are not part of the work end up breathing it in.

Even short runs of chasing can contaminate large areas if dust control and containment are poor. Complaints from occupants and re-cleaning often follow, and what looks like a quick job becomes a disruption problem.

## What “good” looks like on wall chasing in occupied buildings

A workable setup for chasing walls indoors has three parts:

**1) The chaser controls dust at source**  
Use a wall chaser with a proper shroud that fully encloses the blades. Open-sided cutting throws dust straight into the room and defeats any extractor you connect to it.

**2) The extractor is matched to the job**  
Use an M-class (or better) extractor with enough airflow to keep up with continuous chasing. Small vacuums and clogged filters quickly lose capture performance.

**3) The room is treated as a work zone**  
Containment matters as much as extraction. Doors closed, gaps sealed, and work kept within a defined area prevents dust drifting into occupied spaces.

---

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls reduce dust spread in real occupied buildings:

**On-tool extraction**  
Shrouded wall chaser connected to an M-class extractor. Keep the hose short and unkinked.

**Local containment**  
Temporary screens or zip walls to isolate the work area from corridors and adjacent rooms.

**Work sequencing**  
Complete all chasing in one session where possible. Avoid spreading dusty work across multiple visits.

**Housekeeping during the job**  
Pause to clear settled dust with an extractor rather than letting it build up and get walked through.

---

## What to avoid (common mistakes)

**Dry chasing with no extraction**  
This fills rooms with respirable dust very quickly.

**Leaving doors open “for access”**  
Open doors become dust highways into occupied areas.

**Overloaded or blocked extraction**  
A full bag or blocked filter quietly turns a good setup into a bad one.

**Dry sweeping after chasing**  
This re-suspends settled dust back into the air and spreads contamination further.

---

## PPE: important, but not the main control

Respiratory protection is still needed for the person doing the chasing, but it does nothing for occupants in nearby rooms. If people outside the work area can smell or taste dust, control at source and containment are not doing their job.

---

## A simple baseline setup (occupied buildings)

If you need a practical starting point:

- Wall chaser with full shroud  
- M-class extractor with clean filter  
- Temporary screen or sealed doorway  
- Clear signage and restricted access  
- Extract settled dust before reopening the area

This is not perfect control. It is enough to prevent most chasing jobs from contaminating surrounding spaces.

---

## UK context

In the UK, employers are expected to control exposure to respirable crystalline silica. On occupied sites, this includes preventing dust from spreading to people not involved in the task. Chasing walls indoors without effective extraction and containment is unlikely to meet what is considered reasonable control on modern sites.

---

## Quick checklist (printable)

- Chaser has a full shroud  
- Extractor is M-class and switched on  
- Doors and openings controlled  
- Dust contained to the work zone  
- Area cleaned before reopening to occupants

If you can tick all five, you are ahead of most occupied-building chasing setups.
