## The problem

Surface grinding concrete floors creates sustained, fine dust over long periods. Unlike short cutting tasks, floor grinding often continues for hours, which means exposure builds steadily if controls aren’t in place.

The dust generated spreads across the room, settles on surfaces, and drifts into adjacent spaces. In refurb jobs, floor grinding often happens before finishing trades arrive, but the dust remains long after the grinders are packed away.

Common ways floor grinding goes wrong:

- Grinding dry with no extraction.
- Using underpowered extractors that can’t keep up with the dust volume.
- Grinding in open spaces with no containment.

The result is fine dust coating large areas and lingering in the air long after work stops.

## What “good” looks like on surface floor grinding

Good control on floor grinding is about sustained dust capture:

- Dust is captured continuously at the grinding head.
- The extractor is matched to the grinder’s output.
- The work area is treated as a dusty zone and separated from clean spaces.

When capture is continuous and reliable, floor grinding can be done with far less spread of dust.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when surface grinding concrete floors:

- **On-tool extraction with proper shrouds**  
  Floor grinders should be fitted with shrouds designed for extraction, not improvised covers.

- **M-class (or better) extractors**  
  High dust volumes require extractors that can maintain airflow without clogging or leaking fine dust.

- **Filter maintenance**  
  Regularly check and clean filters. Clogged filters quickly turn good setups into bad ones.

- **Zoning and containment**  
  Separate grinding areas from adjacent rooms using screens or sealed doorways.

- **Staged grinding and cleanup**  
  Break work into sections and vacuum settled dust between stages.

## What to avoid

- **Dry grinding with no extraction**  
  This fills rooms with fine dust quickly.

- **Using domestic vacuums**  
  They clog, leak, and re-release fine dust.

- **Grinding across large open areas with no separation**  
  Dust spreads into the whole building.

- **Ignoring filter condition**  
  Blocked filters drastically reduce extraction performance.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection is important for the person grinding, especially during prolonged floor prep. It doesn’t protect others in the space or stop dust settling on surfaces.

If visibility drops during grinding, improving extraction and containment will reduce exposure more effectively than relying on PPE alone.

## A simple baseline setup

For most surface floor grinding jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Floor grinder with extraction shroud
- M-class extractor matched to grinder size
- Filters checked and cleaned regularly
- Temporary screens separating the work area
- Regular vacuuming between grinding stages

This setup keeps sustained grinding work from contaminating the rest of the building.

## UK context 

In the UK, prolonged grinding of concrete floors is recognised as a high dust activity and expected to be controlled with effective extraction and containment. Using underpowered or inappropriate equipment is unlikely to be considered reasonable control for sustained grinding work.

If dust is visible in the air during floor grinding, the controls are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- Grinder shroud fitted  
- M-class extractor connected  
- Filters clean  
- Work area separated  
- Dust vacuumed between stages  

Ticking these five keeps most floor grinding jobs under control.
