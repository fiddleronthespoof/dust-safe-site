## The problem

Breaking out wall chases creates concentrated bursts of fine dust. The chase has usually already been cut, but the breakout phase throws dust into the room as material is chipped away. Because the heavy cutting is “done,” controls are often relaxed at this stage.

The breakout dust drifts into the room, settles on surfaces, and spreads into adjacent spaces. In occupied buildings, this is often the stage that causes the most complaints because the dust cloud feels sudden and uncontrolled.

Common ways breaking out chases goes wrong:

- Chipping out chases dry with no dust suppression.
- Removing material in open rooms with no separation.
- Leaving debris and dust in the chase area to be disturbed later.

The result is a burst of dust that spreads far beyond the wall itself.

## What “good” looks like on breaking out chases

Good control on chase breakout is about treating it as a dusty task in its own right:

- Dust is suppressed or captured during breakout, not just during cutting.
- The work area is separated from clean spaces.
- Debris is removed and cleaned up as part of the breakout process.

When breakout is treated as a dusty stage, the overall impact of chasing work drops noticeably.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when breaking out wall chases:

- **Light damping down**  
  Lightly dampening the chase before breakout reduces airborne dust without creating slurry.

- **Local extraction**  
  Use a vacuum nozzle held close to the breakout point to capture dust as material is removed.

- **Local containment**  
  Close doors or use temporary screens to stop dust drifting into adjacent rooms.

- **Staged breakout and cleanup**  
  Break out in short sections and vacuum debris before moving along the chase.

- **Debris handling**  
  Bag or cover removed material promptly to stop it shedding dust later.

## What to avoid

- **Dry chipping in finished rooms**  
  This sends dust across the room quickly.

- **Sweeping out chases dry**  
  Sweeping re-suspends fine dust into the air.

- **Leaving broken material exposed**  
  Debris continues to shed dust when disturbed.

- **Assuming the cutting stage was the only dusty part**  
  Breakout often creates just as much airborne dust.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection helps the person breaking out the chase but doesn’t protect others nearby or stop dust settling on surfaces.

If dust visibly fills the room during breakout, improving damping, capture, and containment will reduce exposure more effectively than relying on PPE alone.

## A simple baseline setup

For most wall chase breakout jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Chase lightly damped before breakout
- Vacuum nozzle used alongside chisel or SDS tool
- Doors/screens in place to contain dust
- Debris bagged promptly
- Area vacuumed during and after breakout

This keeps chase breakout from becoming the dustiest part of the job.

## UK context 

In the UK, controlling silica dust from breaking out masonry and concrete is expected wherever reasonably practicable, especially indoors. The expectation is that sensible, proportionate controls are used during all stages of the work, not just the cutting stage.

If dust is spreading beyond the work area, the controls are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- Chase damped before breakout  
- Vacuum capture used during chipping  
- Doors/screens in place  
- Debris bagged promptly  
- Area vacuumed after breakout  

Ticking these five keeps most chase breakout jobs under control.
