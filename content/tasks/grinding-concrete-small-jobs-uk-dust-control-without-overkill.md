## The problem

Small grinding jobs create a lot of fine dust for the amount of time they take. A quick tidy-up, a door trim, a small patch grind. Because the job feels minor, the controls often are too.

The dust doesn’t care whether the job is “small.” The particles are the same size, and they hang in the air just as long.

Common ways small grinding goes wrong:

- The grinder has no shroud fitted.
- The extractor is “somewhere nearby” but not actually connected.
- The job is treated as too quick to bother setting up properly.

The result is usually a room that looks clean but smells dusty, and a cloud that drifts into spaces where no one agreed to breathe it in.

## What “good” looks like on a small grinding job

A sensible setup for small grinding work doesn’t need to be elaborate. It just needs to be deliberate:

- The grinder is fitted with a dust shroud.
- The extractor is connected and switched on before the disc touches concrete.
- The space is treated as a space that will fill with dust unless you control it.

Good control on small jobs is about habits, not hardware.

## Practical controls that actually help

These are the controls that make a noticeable difference on small grinding work:

- **On-tool extraction**  
  A shrouded grinder connected to an M-class extractor catches most dust at source. This is the single biggest improvement you can make.

- **Short hose runs**  
  Long, coiled hoses reduce airflow. Keep the extractor close to the work so it can actually pull dust away.

- **Local containment**  
  Closing doors or putting up a simple temporary screen stops dust drifting into occupied areas.

- **Clean as you go**  
  Vacuuming settled dust between stages stops it being kicked back into the air by boots, trolleys, or air movement.

- **Disc choice**  
  Worn or inappropriate grinding discs often create more dust and take longer to cut, increasing exposure time.

## What to avoid

- **“Just a quick grind” with no extraction**  
  This is how small jobs quietly become regular dust exposure.

- **Domestic vacuums**  
  They clog, leak fine dust, and give the impression of control without much of the effect.

- **Letting filters block up**  
  A half-blocked extractor is often worse than none at all because people think they’re protected.

- **Grinding in occupied spaces with no separation**  
  If other people are working or living nearby, dust control needs to account for them, not just the person on the tool.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Masks and respirators protect the person doing the grinding. They don’t protect everyone else in the room, and they don’t stop dust settling on surfaces.

If you’re relying on PPE because the dust cloud is obvious, control at source has already failed.

## A simple baseline setup

For most small grinding jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Grinder fitted with a dust shroud
- M-class extractor with clean filter
- Short hose run with no tight bends
- Door closed or simple screen in place
- Quick vacuum of the area before moving on

This setup won’t eliminate dust completely, but it will keep it to a level most sites consider reasonable for small tasks.

## UK context

In the UK, silica dust is a known health risk and expected to be controlled where reasonably practicable. “Small job” is not an exemption. The expectation is that you use available, proportionate controls rather than defaulting to dry grinding and a mask.

If you wouldn’t accept a room full of dust at the end of the job, the controls probably weren’t good enough during it either.
