## The problem

Breaking out small openings in masonry walls creates bursts of heavy dust and falling debris. Door widenings, service penetrations, and small knock-throughs often happen in enclosed or finished spaces, where dust spreads quickly.

Because the opening is “small,” controls are often minimal. The work is done dry, in open rooms, with cleanup left until the end. The dust cloud is intense, and it travels far beyond the wall being opened.

Common ways small wall breakouts go wrong:

- Dry breaking with no dust suppression.
- Working in open rooms with no separation.
- Letting debris accumulate during the breakout.

The result is a room filled with fine dust that takes far longer to clean than the breakout took to do.

## What “good” looks like on small wall openings

Good control on wall breakouts is about managing dust and debris as part of the task:

- Dust is suppressed or captured during breaking.
- The work area is separated from clean spaces.
- Debris is removed and cleaned up as the opening is formed.

When the breakout is controlled, the mess stays local to the opening instead of spreading across the room.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when breaking out small wall openings:

- **Light damping down**  
  Lightly dampen the work area before and during breaking to reduce airborne dust.

- **Local extraction**  
  Hold a vacuum nozzle close to the breakout point to capture dust as material is removed.

- **Temporary containment**  
  Use screens or sealed doorways to keep dust from drifting into adjacent rooms.

- **Staged breakout and debris removal**  
  Break out in sections and bag debris promptly instead of letting it pile up.

- **Surface protection**  
  Protect nearby surfaces and floors from falling debris and settled dust.

## What to avoid

- **Dry breaking in finished rooms**  
  This fills clean spaces with dust very quickly.

- **Sweeping debris dry**  
  Sweeping re-launches fine dust into the air. Vacuum or damp clean instead.

- **Letting rubble sit uncovered**  
  Broken masonry sheds dust when disturbed.

- **Assuming small openings don’t create big mess**  
  Dust clouds don’t scale down neatly with the size of the opening.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection helps the person breaking out the wall but doesn’t protect others in the room or stop dust settling on surfaces.

If visibility drops during the breakout, improving damping, capture, and containment will reduce exposure more effectively than relying on PPE alone.

## A simple baseline setup

For most small masonry wall breakout jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Work area lightly damped
- Vacuum nozzle used alongside breaking tool
- Temporary screens or separation in place
- Debris bagged or covered promptly
- Area vacuumed during and after breakout

This keeps wall breakouts from contaminating the entire room.

## UK context 

In the UK, controlling dust from breaking masonry and concrete is expected wherever reasonably practicable, especially indoors and in occupied spaces. Small openings are not exempt from this expectation.

If dust is spreading into adjacent rooms or lingering in the air, the controls are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- Area damped before breaking  
- Vacuum capture used during breakout  
- Screens/separation in place  
- Debris bagged promptly  
- Area vacuumed after work  

Ticking these five keeps most small wall openings under control.
