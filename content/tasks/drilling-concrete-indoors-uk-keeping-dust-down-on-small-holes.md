## The problem

Drilling small holes indoors feels harmless. A few fixings, a couple of anchors, done in minutes. The dust is fine, light, and easy to ignore.

But the dust from drilling concrete still contains silica. It settles on surfaces, drifts into other rooms, and gets breathed in by anyone nearby. Because the work is quick, controls are often skipped. Over time, lots of “small” jobs add up.

Common ways indoor drilling goes wrong:

- The drill has no dust capture at all.
- The extractor exists but isn’t used for short tasks.
- Holes are drilled in occupied rooms with no thought to where the dust goes.

## What “good” looks like on indoor drilling

Good control on drilling isn’t about slowing the job down. It’s about building one small habit into every hole:

- The dust is captured at the point of drilling.
- The room is treated as a space that shouldn’t be filled with dust.
- The controls are set up once and reused for every hole, instead of being optional extras.

When dust capture becomes part of the drilling setup, not an add-on, exposure drops without much impact on speed.

## Practical controls that actually help

These are the controls that make a noticeable difference for indoor drilling:

- **On-tool dust capture**  
  Use drill-mounted dust collectors or shrouds that catch dust at the hole. Even simple attachments dramatically reduce the dust plume.

- **Extractor connection**  
  Connecting a small extractor to the drill attachment keeps fine dust from escaping into the room. Short hose runs help maintain airflow.

- **Work positioning**  
  Drill so gravity helps you. Dust falls downward. Capturing it close to the hole is easier when you’re not fighting airflow.

- **Local containment**  
  Closing doors or using a simple screen stops dust drifting into adjacent spaces, especially in occupied buildings.

- **Vacuum between holes**  
  A quick pass over settled dust prevents it being walked through the building and re-suspended later.

## What to avoid

- **Dry drilling with no capture**  
  This creates a visible dust plume with every hole.

- **Assuming “it’s only a few holes”**  
  Repeated small exposures add up across days and weeks.

- **Using worn drill bits**  
  Dull bits take longer to cut, increasing dust generation and exposure time.

- **Drilling in occupied rooms without separation**  
  If people are working or living nearby, dust control has to account for them as well.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection helps the person drilling. It doesn’t protect everyone else in the room and doesn’t stop dust settling on surfaces.

If you’re relying on a mask because dust is clearly visible, control at source is already lacking.

## A simple baseline setup

For most indoor drilling tasks, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Drill with a dust capture attachment
- Small M-class extractor connected
- Short hose run with no tight bends
- Door closed or simple screen in place
- Quick vacuum of the area before leaving

This setup is quick to deploy and doesn’t slow the job much once it becomes routine.

## UK context 

In the UK, silica dust is a recognised health risk and expected to be controlled where reasonably practicable. Drilling is often overlooked because the dust volume seems small, but the expectation is still that sensible, proportionate controls are used indoors.

If dust is visible in the air during drilling, the controls are probably below what would be considered reasonable on a modern site.

## Quick checklist

- Drill has dust capture fitted
- Extractor connected and running
- Doors/screens in place
- Bits sharp and cutting cleanly
- Area vacuumed before moving on

Ticking these five keeps most small indoor drilling jobs on the right side of “reasonable control.”
