## The problem

Edge grinding focuses dust into a small area close to the operator’s breathing zone. The work often happens along walls, thresholds, and corners where airflow is poor and dust has nowhere to go.

Because edge grinding is seen as “detail work,” extraction is sometimes treated as optional. The grinder gets run dry for short bursts, and dust builds up along edges and skirting lines.

Common ways edge grinding goes wrong:

- Dry grinding along walls with no extraction.
- Using shrouds that don’t fit properly in tight corners.
- Grinding in finished spaces with no separation.

The result is fine dust concentrated exactly where people are working and moving.

## What “good” looks like on edge grinding

Good control on edge grinding is about maintaining dust capture even in awkward spaces:

- Extraction stays connected for edge work, not just open floor areas.
- Shrouds and attachments are chosen to fit close to walls and corners.
- The work area is treated as a dusty zone and separated from clean spaces.

When edge grinding is set up properly, detail work doesn’t undo the dust control achieved on open floor grinding.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when edge grinding concrete:

- **Edge-capable shrouds**  
  Use shrouds designed to get close to walls and corners while maintaining extraction.

- **M-class extraction**  
  Edge grinding produces fine dust at close range. Effective extraction is essential.

- **Work sequencing**  
  Complete edge grinding in one session rather than dipping in and out, then clean thoroughly.

- **Local containment**  
  Close doors or use screens to stop dust drifting into adjacent rooms.

- **Vacuum along edges**  
  Regularly vacuum dust buildup along walls to stop it being walked through the space.

## What to avoid

- **Removing shrouds for access**  
  This creates concentrated dust right at face level.

- **Using worn grinding discs**  
  Dull discs generate more dust and take longer to cut.

- **Grinding in finished rooms with no separation**  
  Fine dust settles on surfaces that are hard to clean later.

- **Letting dust build up along edges**  
  Edge dust gets kicked back into the air by foot traffic.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection is important during edge grinding, particularly because the dust is generated close to the breathing zone. It doesn’t protect others nearby or stop dust settling on surfaces.

If dust is clearly visible around the grinder head, improving shrouds and extraction will usually reduce exposure more effectively than relying on PPE alone.

## A simple baseline setup

For most edge grinding tasks, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Edge-compatible shroud fitted to grinder
- M-class extractor connected
- Doors/screens in place to contain dust
- Edge areas vacuumed regularly during work
- Final vacuum before moving to the next area

This setup keeps edge grinding from undermining the rest of the dust control plan.

## UK context

In the UK, edge grinding of concrete is expected to be controlled in the same way as other high dust grinding tasks. The expectation is that effective extraction and sensible containment are used to limit exposure and prevent dust spreading into adjacent spaces.

If fine dust is accumulating along walls and in corners, the controls are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- Edge shroud fitted  
- M-class extractor connected  
- Doors/screens in place  
- Edges vacuumed during work  
- Final clean-down completed  

Ticking these five keeps most edge grinding jobs under control.
