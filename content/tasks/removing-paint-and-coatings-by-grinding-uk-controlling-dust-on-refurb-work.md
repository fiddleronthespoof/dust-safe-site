## The problem

Grinding off paint, sealers, and coatings from concrete creates fine dust mixed with fragments of the coating itself. This dust can linger in the air and settle across large areas, especially indoors or in partially enclosed spaces.

Because the aim is to “clean up” a surface, dust control is often overlooked. The focus goes to getting the coating off quickly, and extraction is treated as optional. The result is a dusty environment that spreads contamination well beyond the grinding area.

Common ways coating removal goes wrong:

- Dry grinding with no extraction.
- Using grinders not designed for surface prep work.
- Working in open spaces with no containment.

The result is fine dust spreading into adjacent rooms and settling on finished surfaces.

## What “good” looks like on coating removal

Good control on coating removal is about sustained capture and containment:

- Dust is captured continuously at the grinding head.
- The work area is separated from clean spaces.
- The grinder and extractor are matched to the task and run as a pair.

When capture is continuous, coating removal doesn’t contaminate the rest of the building.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when grinding off paint and coatings:

- **On-tool extraction with proper shrouds**  
  Surface prep grinders should be fitted with shrouds designed for coating removal, not improvised covers.

- **M-class (or better) extractors**  
  Coating dust is fine and persistent. Extractors must maintain airflow and filtration throughout the job.

- **Filter maintenance**  
  Coating dust clogs filters quickly. Check and clean filters regularly to maintain extraction performance.

- **Zoning and containment**  
  Separate the grinding area from adjacent spaces using screens or sealed doorways.

- **Staged grinding and cleanup**  
  Work in sections and vacuum settled dust between stages to prevent buildup.

## What to avoid

- **Dry grinding with no extraction**  
  This spreads coating dust across the building quickly.

- **Using domestic vacuums**  
  They clog, leak, and re-release fine dust.

- **Grinding in open spaces with no separation**  
  Dust drifts into clean areas and finished rooms.

- **Ignoring filter condition**  
  Blocked filters drastically reduce dust capture.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection is important during coating removal, particularly because fine dust and coating residues are generated continuously. PPE does not prevent dust settling on surfaces or spreading into adjacent rooms.

If dust is visibly lingering in the air, improving extraction and containment will reduce exposure more effectively than relying on PPE alone.

## A simple baseline setup

For most coating removal by grinding, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Surface prep grinder with extraction shroud
- M-class extractor matched to grinder size
- Filters checked and cleaned regularly
- Temporary screens separating the work area
- Regular vacuuming between grinding stages

This setup keeps coating removal work from contaminating the rest of the space.

## UK context

In the UK, removing coatings from concrete by grinding is treated as a high dust activity and expected to be controlled with effective extraction and sensible containment. Using underpowered or inappropriate equipment is unlikely to be considered reasonable control for sustained coating removal work.

If dust is spreading beyond the immediate work area, the controls are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- Grinder shroud fitted  
- M-class extractor connected  
- Filters clean  
- Work area separated  
- Dust vacuumed between stages  

Ticking these five keeps most coating removal jobs under control.
