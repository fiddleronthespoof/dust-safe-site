## The problem

Cutting concrete kerbs is heavy, repetitive work. The kerbs are dense, the cuts are long, and the dust generated is persistent. Because this work is often done outdoors on highways or hardstanding, dust control can slip into “good enough” mode.

The dust plume from kerb cutting drifts across work areas, into passing traffic, and toward nearby buildings. Slurry from wet cutting spreads across pavements and roads if it’s not managed. The combination creates both dust and slip hazards in public-facing spaces.

Common ways kerb cutting goes wrong:

- Dry cutting with no suppression because it’s outside.
- Wet cutting without managing slurry runoff.
- Cutting near pedestrians or open doorways without separation.

The result is dust drifting into areas it shouldn’t, and slurry turning into secondary mess and hazards.

## What “good” looks like on kerb cutting

Good control on kerb cutting is about managing both dust and slurry in open, public-facing environments:

- Dust is suppressed at source.
- Slurry is captured or contained instead of being allowed to spread.
- The work area is positioned and managed to limit impact on others.

When both dust and slurry are treated as part of the task, kerb cutting becomes safer and less disruptive.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when cutting concrete kerbs:

- **Wet cutting with slurry control**  
  Wet cutting suppresses dust effectively. Use trays, mats, or temporary channels to control where slurry flows.

- **On-tool extraction for dry cuts**  
  Where wet cutting isn’t practical, shrouds and extractors reduce the dust plume at source.

- **Positioning and separation**  
  Set up cutting away from pedestrian routes, doors, and building openings. Use barriers to keep people clear of dust and slurry.

- **Wind awareness**  
  Wind direction affects where dust drifts. Position cuts so plumes move away from people and occupied spaces.

- **Staged cutting and cleanup**  
  Break long cuts into stages and clean slurry and settled dust as you go to prevent buildup.

## What to avoid

- **Dry cutting in public areas with no control**  
  This exposes passers-by to dust unnecessarily.

- **Letting slurry run across pavements or roads**  
  Slurry creates slip hazards and dries into dust later.

- **Cutting next to building entrances**  
  Dust and slurry get tracked straight inside.

- **Ignoring wind direction**  
  Even light wind can carry dust into occupied areas.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection is important for the person cutting, especially during prolonged kerb cuts. It doesn’t protect passers-by or prevent slurry spreading.

In public-facing work, control at source and separation matter as much as personal protection.

## A simple baseline setup

For most concrete kerb cutting jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Wet cutting used with slurry controlled
- Barriers or separation in place to keep people clear
- Cutting positioned away from entrances and pedestrian routes
- Wind direction considered before starting
- Slurry and settled dust cleaned up during the job

This keeps kerb cutting from becoming a nuisance or hazard to others.

## UK context 

In the UK, controlling silica dust from cutting concrete products is expected wherever reasonably practicable, including on highways and public-facing work. The expectation is that dust and slurry are managed to prevent harm to workers and members of the public.

If dust is drifting across pavements or slurry is creating slip hazards, the controls are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- Wet cutting or extraction in use  
- Slurry controlled  
- Barriers in place  
- Wind direction considered  
- Area cleaned during and after work  

Ticking these five keeps most kerb cutting jobs contained and safer for everyone around.
