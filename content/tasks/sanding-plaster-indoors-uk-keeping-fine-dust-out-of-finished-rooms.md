## The problem

Sanding plaster indoors creates very fine dust. It’s light, it floats, and it settles everywhere. On finished jobs, that dust doesn’t just affect the person sanding. It coats surfaces, drifts into adjacent rooms, and ends up in places that are hard to clean properly.

Because sanding feels like “finishing work,” dust control is often an afterthought. People open a window, hold their breath for a few minutes, and carry on. The room looks clean later, but the dust has already spread.

Common ways plaster sanding goes wrong:

- Dry sanding with no dust capture.
- Sanding in finished rooms with no containment.
- Leaving cleanup until the very end.

The result is fine dust in corners, on ledges, and in soft furnishings that weren’t meant to be part of the job.

## What “good” looks like on indoor plaster sanding

Good control on plaster sanding is about keeping the dust where the sanding happens:

- The sanding tool captures dust at source.
- The room is treated as a contained space while sanding is happening.
- Dust is removed as it’s created, not just after the job is done.

When dust capture is part of the sanding setup, finished rooms stay closer to finished.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when sanding plaster indoors:

- **On-tool extraction**  
  Use sanding tools with integrated dust capture connected to an extractor. This catches most dust before it becomes airborne.

- **Dustless sanding systems**  
  Purpose-made “dustless” sanders are designed to keep fine plaster dust under control. They’re not perfect, but they’re far better than dry hand sanding.

- **Local containment**  
  Close doors and use temporary screens to keep dust from drifting into other rooms.

- **Surface protection**  
  Cover nearby surfaces and furniture. Fine dust settles on everything.

- **Clean as you go**  
  Vacuum between sanding stages. This stops settled dust being kicked back into the air.

## What to avoid

- **Dry hand sanding with no extraction**  
  This fills the room with fine dust quickly.

- **Relying on ventilation alone**  
  Open windows move dust around. They don’t capture it.

- **Using brooms to clean up**  
  Sweeping stirs settled dust back into the air. Vacuum or damp wipe instead.

- **Sanding with other trades working nearby**  
  Dust affects anyone in the space, not just the person sanding.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection helps the person sanding, especially with fine plaster dust. It doesn’t stop dust settling on surfaces or drifting into adjacent rooms.

If the room needs a full deep clean after sanding, control at source and containment during the work were likely not sufficient.

## A simple baseline setup

For most indoor plaster sanding jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Sander with dust capture connected to an extractor
- Doors closed or temporary screens in place
- Nearby surfaces covered
- Regular vacuuming during the sanding process
- Final vacuum and damp wipe before handover

This setup takes a little longer to prepare but saves time on cleanup and complaints later.

## UK context 

In the UK, fine dust from building materials is expected to be controlled where reasonably practicable, especially indoors and in finished spaces. Plaster dust may not contain silica like concrete dust, but it still causes respiratory irritation and widespread contamination of finished areas.

Keeping sanding dust under control is part of reasonable site practice in occupied or finished environments.

## Quick checklist

- Dust capture attached to sander
- Extractor running
- Doors/screens in place
- Surfaces protected
- Area vacuumed and wiped before leaving

Ticking these five keeps most indoor sanding jobs from turning clean rooms into dusty ones.
