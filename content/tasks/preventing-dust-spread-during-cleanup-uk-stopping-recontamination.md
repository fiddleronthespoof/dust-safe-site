## The problem

Cleanup is often the moment when dust spreads the most. Walking through contaminated areas, moving tools, and opening doors stirs settled dust back into the air and carries it into clean spaces.

Even well-controlled dusty work can be undone by careless cleanup. Dust gets tracked on boots, transferred on tools, and re-suspended by sweeping and movement. The building ends up dusty in areas that were never part of the work zone.

Common ways dust spread happens during cleanup:

- Walking dust through the building on boots and wheels.
- Carrying dusty tools and materials into clean areas.
- Sweeping dry debris instead of capturing it.

The result is contamination spreading during the very phase meant to remove it.

## What “good” looks like during cleanup

Good cleanup practice is about containing contamination until it’s removed:

- Dusty areas are treated as zones until fully cleaned.
- Tools and people move from dirty to clean in a controlled way.
- Dust is captured, not redistributed, during cleanup.

When cleanup is controlled, the dust problem shrinks instead of spreading.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference in preventing dust spread during cleanup:

- **Zoning and routes**  
  Define dirty and clean zones and limit movement between them until cleanup is complete.

- **Boot cleaning**  
  Use sticky mats or boot cleaning before leaving dusty zones.

- **Tool wipe-down**  
  Wipe or vacuum tools before moving them into clean areas.

- **Vacuum before sweeping**  
  Capture loose dust with a vacuum before any wiping or mopping.

- **Progressive cleanup**  
  Clean in stages from the work area outward, rather than moving dust through clean spaces.

## What to avoid

- **Walking straight from dusty areas into clean rooms**  
  This tracks contamination into spaces that were not part of the work.

- **Dry sweeping across thresholds**  
  This pushes dust into adjacent rooms.

- **Carrying dusty tools through finished areas**  
  Tools shed dust as they move.

- **Opening multiple doors and routes during cleanup**  
  Air movement carries dust into new areas.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection can be useful during cleanup in dusty environments. It doesn’t prevent dust being transferred to other spaces.

If cleanup visibly stirs dust into the air, the method should be adjusted to capture dust rather than move it.

## A simple baseline setup

For most site cleanups after dusty work, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Dirty and clean zones identified
- Sticky mats or boot cleaning at exits
- Tools vacuumed or wiped before moving
- Vacuum used before any sweeping or wiping
- Cleanup staged from dirty to clean areas

This prevents cleanup from becoming a second dust-spreading phase.

## UK context

In the UK, preventing the spread of hazardous dust beyond the work area is part of reasonable site control. Allowing dust to be tracked into clean or occupied areas during cleanup is likely to result in additional exposure and complaints.

If dust appears in areas that were not part of the work zone, the cleanup process needs tightening.

## Quick checklist

- Zones defined  
- Boots cleaned before exiting dusty areas  
- Tools wiped or vacuumed  
- Vacuum used before sweeping  
- Cleanup staged from dirty to clean  

Ticking these five stops cleanup from undoing earlier dust control.
