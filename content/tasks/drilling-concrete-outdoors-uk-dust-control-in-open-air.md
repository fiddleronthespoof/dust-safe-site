## The problem

Drilling concrete outdoors often feels low-risk because the dust “blows away.” In reality, dust still forms a cloud around the drill point and drifts toward nearby people, vehicles, and open buildings.

Because it’s outside, controls are often skipped. Small holes get drilled dry, extraction stays in the van, and wind direction is ignored. The dust plume may be less obvious than indoors, but it’s still there.

Common ways outdoor drilling goes wrong:

- Drilling dry with no dust capture because it’s “just a few holes.”
- Drilling near open doors, windows, or vents.
- Standing downwind of the drilling point.

The result is dust drifting into places it doesn’t belong and being breathed in by people who aren’t part of the task.

## What “good” looks like on outdoor drilling

Good control outdoors is about reducing the dust plume at the point of drilling and thinking about where that plume goes:

- Dust is captured at the hole.
- The drilling position is chosen to limit drift toward people or buildings.
- Controls are used even when the job feels small.

This keeps outdoor drilling from quietly becoming repeated low-level exposure.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when drilling concrete outdoors:

- **On-tool dust capture**  
  Drill-mounted dust collectors or shrouds catch dust at the hole instead of letting it drift.

- **Extractor connection**  
  Connecting a small extractor reduces the visible dust plume, even outdoors.

- **Positioning and wind awareness**  
  Stand upwind of the drilling point and position the work so dust drifts away from people and openings.

- **Distance from buildings**  
  Drill away from open doors and windows to prevent dust being drawn indoors.

- **Vacuum between holes**  
  A quick vacuum of settled dust stops it being kicked up by foot traffic.

## What to avoid

- **Assuming outdoor air equals control**  
  Air movement spreads dust. It doesn’t remove it.

- **Drilling next to open buildings**  
  Dust gets drawn inside.

- **Standing downwind of the hole**  
  This puts the dust plume straight in your breathing zone.

- **Letting dust settle and get walked through**  
  Settled dust gets re-suspended easily.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection helps the person drilling but doesn’t protect others nearby or stop dust settling on surfaces.

If dust is visible around the drill point, improving capture at source will usually reduce exposure more than relying on PPE alone.

## A simple baseline setup

For most outdoor drilling jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Drill with dust capture attachment
- Small extractor connected where practical
- Drilling position chosen with wind in mind
- Work positioned away from doors and windows
- Area vacuumed after drilling

This setup keeps most outdoor drilling from becoming a nuisance to others or drifting indoors.

## UK context 

In the UK, controlling silica dust from drilling concrete is expected wherever reasonably practicable, indoors and outdoors. The fact that work is outside doesn’t remove the expectation to use sensible, proportionate controls.

If dust is drifting into buildings or across occupied areas, the controls are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- Dust capture fitted to drill  
- Extractor used where practical  
- Wind direction considered  
- Drilling positioned away from openings  
- Area vacuumed after work  

Ticking these five keeps most outdoor drilling jobs contained.
