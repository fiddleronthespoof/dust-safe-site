## The problem

Cutting lintels and precast concrete often involves deeper cuts, denser material, and longer cutting times than routine masonry work. The dust generated is heavier, more persistent, and more likely to spread beyond the immediate work area.

These cuts are frequently done near openings, doorways, and structural interfaces, which means dust has a clear path into adjacent rooms and occupied spaces. Because the work is structural, attention is often on the cut itself, not on where the dust goes.

Common ways lintel and precast cutting goes wrong:

- Dry cutting with no suppression because wet cutting feels inconvenient.
- Cutting near open doorways or windows with no separation.
- Continuous cutting without breaks, creating prolonged dust clouds.

The result is dust spreading into spaces that were meant to stay clean and safe for others.

## What “good” looks like on lintel and precast cutting

Good control on structural cutting is about planning the cut and the dust together:

- Dust is suppressed at source wherever possible.
- The cut is positioned to limit drift into adjacent spaces.
- The work area is treated as a dusty zone and separated from clean areas.

When dust control is part of the cutting plan, structural cuts can be done without turning the surrounding space into a dust field.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when cutting lintels and precast concrete:

- **Wet cutting where appropriate**  
  Wet cutting suppresses dust effectively on dense concrete products. Manage slurry and runoff to avoid slip hazards and secondary mess.

- **On-tool extraction**  
  Shrouds and extractors reduce the dust plume for dry cuts, particularly indoors or in partially enclosed spaces.

- **Local containment**  
  Use temporary screens or sealed doorways to keep dust from drifting into adjacent rooms.

- **Cut positioning**  
  Where possible, cut away from openings and airflow paths that pull dust into occupied spaces.

- **Staged cutting**  
  Break longer cuts into stages to limit continuous dust generation and allow dust to settle.

## What to avoid

- **Dry cutting in enclosed spaces with no control**  
  This creates prolonged dust clouds that are hard to contain.

- **Cutting directly next to open doorways or windows**  
  Dust is drawn straight into other areas.

- **Allowing slurry to spread unchecked**  
  Slurry dries into dust later if not managed properly.

- **Letting debris and dust accumulate during the job**  
  Settled dust is easily re-suspended by movement.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection is important for the person cutting, especially on deeper, longer cuts. It doesn’t protect others nearby and doesn’t stop dust settling on surfaces.

If dust is visibly hanging in the air during cutting, control at source and containment should be improved before relying on PPE alone.

## A simple baseline setup

For most lintel and precast cutting jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Wet cutting or extraction used at source
- Temporary screens or separation in place
- Cut positioned away from openings
- Slurry and debris managed during the job
- Area cleaned before moving on

This setup keeps structural cutting work from spreading dust beyond the immediate work zone.

## UK context 

In the UK, controlling silica dust from cutting concrete products is expected wherever reasonably practicable. Structural work is not exempt from this expectation. The expectation is that sensible, proportionate controls are used to limit dust at source and prevent it affecting others.

If dust is drifting into adjacent rooms or remaining visible in the air, the controls are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- Wet cutting or extraction in use  
- Area separated from adjacent spaces  
- Cut positioned away from openings  
- Slurry and debris managed  
- Area cleaned after cutting  

If you can tick all five, most lintel and precast cutting jobs stay contained and manageable.
