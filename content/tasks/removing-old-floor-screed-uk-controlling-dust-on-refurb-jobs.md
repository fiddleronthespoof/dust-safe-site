## The problem

Removing old floor screed creates fine dust and large volumes of debris. The work is often done indoors during refurbishments, where dust spreads easily into adjacent rooms and through buildings.

Because screed removal is heavy, physical work, attention often goes to speed and access rather than dust control. The result is prolonged dust generation, with fine particles settling across the space and into areas that weren’t meant to be part of the work zone.

Common ways screed removal goes wrong:

- Breaking out screed dry with no suppression.
- Working across large open areas with no containment.
- Letting debris and dust accumulate before cleanup.

The result is a building filled with fine dust that takes longer to clean than the breakout took to complete.

## What “good” looks like on screed removal

Good control on screed removal is about planning the work so dust doesn’t spread:

- Dust is suppressed or captured during breakout.
- The work area is separated from clean spaces.
- Debris is removed and the area cleaned as the work progresses.

When dust control is built into the method, screed removal becomes more manageable and less disruptive to surrounding spaces.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when removing old floor screed:

- **Light damping down**  
  Lightly dampening the screed surface before and during breakout reduces airborne dust.

- **Local extraction**  
  Use vacuum extraction alongside breakers or chisels where practical to capture dust at source.

- **Zoning and containment**  
  Work in defined zones and separate the screed removal area from adjacent rooms using screens or sealed doorways.

- **Staged breakout and cleanup**  
  Remove screed in sections and vacuum debris regularly to prevent dust buildup.

- **Debris handling**  
  Bag or cover broken screed promptly. Open piles of debris continue to shed dust when disturbed.

## What to avoid

- **Dry breaking in open buildings**  
  This fills adjacent rooms with dust quickly.

- **Sweeping dry debris**  
  Sweeping re-suspends fine dust into the air. Vacuum or damp clean instead.

- **Letting dust build up across the work area**  
  Accumulated dust is easily kicked back into the air.

- **Working across large areas without staging**  
  This creates continuous dust clouds over longer periods.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection is important during screed removal, especially for prolonged breakout. It doesn’t protect others nearby or stop dust settling on surfaces.

If dust visibly fills the space for long periods, improving damping, capture, and containment will reduce exposure more effectively than relying on PPE alone.

## A simple baseline setup

For most floor screed removal jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Screed lightly damped before and during breakout
- Local extraction used where compatible with tools
- Temporary screens or separation in place
- Debris bagged or covered promptly
- Area vacuumed during and after removal

This setup keeps screed removal from contaminating the rest of the building.

## UK context 

In the UK, controlling dust from breaking concrete-based materials is expected wherever reasonably practicable, particularly indoors and during refurbishments. Old screed often contains fine materials that become airborne easily when broken.

If dust is spreading into adjacent rooms or lingering in the air, the controls are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- Screed damped before breakout  
- Extraction used where practical  
- Work area separated  
- Debris bagged promptly  
- Area vacuumed during and after work  

Ticking these five keeps most screed removal jobs under control.
