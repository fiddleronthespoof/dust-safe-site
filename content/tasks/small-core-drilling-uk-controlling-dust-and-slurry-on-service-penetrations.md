## The problem

Small core drilling is often used for service penetrations: pipes, cables, vents. The holes are larger than typical drill holes, the drilling time is longer, and the mess is greater. Dust and slurry are both part of the process.

Indoors, slurry runs across floors and dust settles on surfaces. Outdoors, slurry spreads across hardstanding and dries into dust later. Because the task sits somewhere between drilling and cutting, controls are often improvised.

Common ways small core drilling goes wrong:

- Dry coring with no dust capture in enclosed spaces.
- Wet coring without any slurry control.
- Drilling through walls or floors with no protection on the far side.

The result is dust and slurry affecting areas that weren’t meant to be part of the work.

## What “good” looks like on small core drilling

Good control on small core drilling is about planning for both dust and slurry:

- Dust is controlled at source if dry coring is used.
- Slurry is captured or contained if wet coring is used.
- Both sides of the penetration are considered before drilling starts.

When the mess is planned for, core drilling doesn’t have to turn into a cleanup operation across multiple rooms.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when small core drilling:

- **Wet coring with slurry control**  
  Wet coring suppresses dust effectively. Use slurry rings, trays, or vacuum attachments to capture runoff.

- **Dry coring with extraction**  
  Where dry coring is used, pair the core drill with suitable extraction to reduce airborne dust.

- **Protection on the far side**  
  Place trays, sheeting, or catchment on the exit side of the hole to catch slurry and debris.

- **Local containment**  
  Indoors, use temporary screens or close doors to stop dust and slurry affecting adjacent spaces.

- **Staged drilling and cleanup**  
  Drill in stages and clean up slurry and settled dust as you go.

## What to avoid

- **Wet coring with no plan for slurry**  
  Uncontrolled slurry spreads across floors and into drains.

- **Dry coring in enclosed spaces with no extraction**  
  This creates prolonged dust clouds.

- **Drilling without protecting the far side**  
  Slurry and debris often cause more mess on the exit side than at the drill face.

- **Letting slurry dry in place**  
  Dried slurry becomes dust later when disturbed.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection is important, especially for dry coring. It doesn’t control where dust and slurry end up. Eye and skin protection also matter due to wet debris and splashing.

If the surrounding area is heavily contaminated with slurry or dust, improving containment and capture at source will usually reduce exposure more effectively than relying on PPE alone.

## A simple baseline setup

For most small core drilling jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Wet coring with slurry capture, or dry coring with extraction
- Trays or sheeting on both sides of the penetration
- Temporary screens or separation in indoor spaces
- Slurry and debris cleaned up during drilling
- Final clean-down before moving to the next area

This setup keeps core drilling from contaminating areas beyond the immediate work zone.

## UK context 

In the UK, controlling silica dust and managing wet debris from concrete work is expected wherever reasonably practicable. Small core drilling is not exempt from this expectation, particularly indoors and in occupied spaces.

If slurry and dust are spreading beyond the work area, the controls are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- Wet coring with slurry control or dry coring with extraction  
- Protection on both sides of the hole  
- Doors/screens in place indoors  
- Slurry and debris cleaned during drilling  
- Final clean-down before moving on  

Ticking these five keeps most small core drilling jobs contained.
