## The problem

Wet cutting and wet coring suppress dust at source, but they create slurry. If slurry isn’t managed, it spreads across floors, pavements, and work areas. Once it dries, it becomes fine dust that’s easily disturbed later.

Because slurry is wet, it’s often treated as less of a problem than dry dust. It gets left to run across surfaces or wash into drains. The mess dries, hardens, and turns into a cleanup and dust problem later in the job.

Common ways slurry management goes wrong:

- Letting slurry run freely across floors or walkways.
- Washing slurry into drains or gullies.
- Leaving slurry to dry on surfaces.

The result is slip hazards in the short term and dust problems in the longer term.

## What “good” looks like on slurry management

Good slurry management is about containment and controlled removal:

- Slurry is captured or directed into a contained area.
- Wet waste is removed before it dries.
- Surfaces are cleaned before slurry hardens into dust.

When slurry is managed as part of the method, wet cutting and coring stay wet work, not delayed dry dust.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when managing slurry and wet waste:

- **Containment mats and trays**  
  Use trays, mats, or temporary channels to keep slurry where it’s generated.

- **Wet vacuums**  
  Use suitable wet vacuums to collect slurry before it spreads or dries.

- **Immediate cleanup**  
  Clean slurry as you go rather than leaving it to harden.

- **Designated disposal**  
  Dispose of slurry and wet waste in line with site arrangements, not into surface drains.

- **Surface protection**  
  Protect finished surfaces from slurry staining and residue.

## What to avoid

- **Letting slurry dry in place**  
  Dried slurry becomes dust later.

- **Washing slurry into drains**  
  This causes blockages and environmental issues.

- **Tracking slurry through buildings**  
  Wet footprints dry into dusty trails.

- **Ignoring slip hazards**  
  Wet slurry creates immediate safety risks.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Gloves and eye protection are useful when handling slurry and wet waste. Respiratory protection is less relevant for wet slurry but becomes relevant once slurry dries and dust is disturbed.

If dried slurry is being swept or disturbed, dust controls should be applied at that stage.

## A simple baseline setup

For most wet cutting and coring jobs, a workable baseline for slurry management looks like this:

- Trays or mats placed under wet work
- Wet vacuum available on hand
- Slurry cleaned up during the task
- Wet waste disposed of in designated areas
- Surfaces cleaned before slurry dries

This keeps wet work from turning into a delayed dust problem.

## UK context

In the UK, managing wet waste from cutting and coring is part of reasonable site practice, both for dust control and environmental protection. Allowing slurry to dry on surfaces or run into drains is unlikely to be considered good practice.

If dried slurry is later being swept or ground off, the slurry management stage was not adequate.

## Quick checklist

- Slurry contained at source  
- Wet vacuum used  
- Cleanup done during the task  
- Disposal routes agreed  
- Surfaces cleaned before drying  

Ticking these five keeps wet work from becoming dry dust later.
