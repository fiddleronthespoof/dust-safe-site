## The problem

Tile cutting indoors often happens in finished or nearly finished rooms. Bathrooms, kitchens, hallways. The work is close to people’s homes or occupied spaces, which makes dust control more important, not less.

Tile dust is finer than it looks. Cutting ceramic, porcelain, or stone tiles releases particles that hang in the air and settle on surfaces people will touch, eat near, and breathe around.

Common ways indoor tile cutting goes wrong:

- Tiles are cut dry in the room where they will be installed.
- The cutter has no dust control, and extraction isn’t considered.
- “It’s only a few cuts” becomes the reason for doing nothing.

The result is a light film of dust over a room that was otherwise finished, and a cloud that drifts into spaces it doesn’t belong.

## What “good” looks like on indoor tile cutting

Good control on tile cutting is about separating the dusty process from the clean space:

- Most cutting is done outside the room, or outside the building if possible.
- If cutting must happen indoors, it’s done in a contained area with controls in place.
- Wet cutting is used deliberately where it makes sense, with proper cleanup.

The goal is to keep dusty work from becoming part of the finished environment.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls reduce dust without turning tile cutting into a major production:

- **Cut tiles away from the finished room**  
  Set up a cutting station in a garage, outside, or in a contained area. Carry finished tiles back to the work area.

- **Wet cutting where appropriate**  
  Wet saws suppress dust effectively, but create slurry. Use trays, mats, and controlled water to avoid spreading mess indoors.

- **Local containment**  
  If cutting indoors is unavoidable, use temporary screens or a separate room to stop dust migrating into clean areas.

- **Vacuum clean-down**  
  Vacuum surfaces and floors before moving tools and materials back into finished spaces.

- **Hand-cutting with capture**  
  For small adjustments, use tile cutters or hand tools that don’t generate airborne dust, where feasible.

## What to avoid

- **Dry cutting tiles in finished rooms**  
  This is one of the fastest ways to coat a clean space in fine dust.

- **Relying on open windows**  
  Ventilation alone doesn’t control where dust settles.

- **Mixing wet slurry and foot traffic**  
  Slurry gets tracked through buildings and dries into dust later if not cleaned properly.

- **Assuming tile dust is “cleaner” than concrete dust**  
  Fine particulate is still fine particulate when it comes to lungs and surfaces.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Masks protect the person cutting. They don’t protect the room. In domestic or finished environments, surface contamination and lingering dust matter as much as personal exposure.

If the room needs a deep clean after cutting, the controls during cutting weren’t good enough.

## A simple baseline setup

For most small indoor tiling jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Primary cutting done outside the room where tiles are installed
- Wet cutter used where practical, with controlled water and slurry
- Temporary screen or separate cutting area if indoors
- Vacuum and wipe-down of surfaces before refitting tiles
- Tools and dusty materials kept out of finished areas

This keeps dusty processes from bleeding into clean spaces.

## UK context 

In the UK, work that creates fine dust indoors is expected to be controlled where reasonably practicable. In domestic and occupied settings, the expectation is higher because the dust affects people who aren’t part of the job.

Keeping tile cutting separate from finished rooms is usually the simplest way to meet that expectation.

## Quick checklist

- Cutting done away from finished room where possible
- Wet cutting used deliberately and cleaned up properly
- Screens or separation in place if cutting indoors
- Surfaces vacuumed and wiped before refitting tiles
- Dusty tools kept out of clean areas

If you can tick all five, most indoor tile cutting jobs stay clean enough to hand over without apology.
