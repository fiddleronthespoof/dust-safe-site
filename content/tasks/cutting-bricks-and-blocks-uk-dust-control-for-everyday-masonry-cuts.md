## The problem

Cutting bricks and blocks is everyday work on UK sites. Door openings, making good, trimming courses. Because it’s routine, dust control often slips into autopilot.

Masonry cutting creates fine dust that lingers in the air and settles on nearby surfaces. Indoors, it drifts into adjacent rooms. Outdoors, it spreads across the work area and into buildings through open doors and windows.

Common ways brick and block cutting goes wrong:

- Dry cutting with no suppression because it’s “only a few cuts.”
- Cutting indoors without separating the work area.
- Cutting near open doors or windows and letting dust drift inside.

The result is dust spread far wider than the cut itself, often into spaces that were meant to stay clean.

## What “good” looks like on brick and block cutting

Good control on masonry cutting is about keeping dust local to the cut:

- Dust is suppressed at source.
- The cutting position is chosen to limit where dust can drift.
- The surrounding area is protected from settled dust and slurry.

This keeps routine cuts from turning into a site-wide clean-up exercise.

## Practical controls that actually help

These controls make a noticeable difference when cutting bricks and blocks:

- **Wet cutting where appropriate**  
  Wet cutting suppresses dust effectively. Manage water and slurry so it doesn’t spread mess or create slip hazards.

- **On-tool extraction**  
  Shrouds and extractors reduce the dust plume for dry cuts, especially indoors.

- **Positioning and separation**  
  Indoors, cut in a contained area or separate the cutting space from clean rooms. Outdoors, position cuts away from doors and windows.

- **Surface protection**  
  Protect nearby finished surfaces from dust and slurry using boards or sheeting.

- **Staged cutting and cleanup**  
  Cut in stages and vacuum or damp clean settled dust before it spreads.

## What to avoid

- **Dry cutting in finished rooms**  
  This coats clean spaces in fine dust very quickly.

- **Cutting next to open doors or windows**  
  Dust gets drawn straight indoors.

- **Letting slurry run across finished surfaces**  
  Slurry dries into dust later if not cleaned properly.

- **Assuming small cuts don’t matter**  
  Repeated small cuts create repeated small dust clouds.

## PPE: where it fits (and where it doesn’t)

Respiratory protection helps the person cutting but doesn’t protect others nearby or stop dust settling on surfaces.

If dust is visible in the air during cutting, controls at source and positioning should be improved before relying on PPE alone.

## A simple baseline setup

For most brick and block cutting jobs, a workable baseline looks like this:

- Wet cutting or extraction used at source
- Cutting position chosen to avoid doors and windows
- Temporary separation if cutting indoors
- Nearby surfaces protected
- Area cleaned after cutting

This setup keeps routine masonry cutting from spreading dust beyond the immediate work area.

## UK context 

In the UK, controlling dust from masonry cutting is expected wherever reasonably practicable, indoors and outdoors. Routine work is not exempt from this expectation. Sensible, proportionate controls should be used to limit dust at source and prevent it affecting others.

If dust is drifting into adjacent rooms or buildings, the controls are likely below what would be considered reasonable.

## Quick checklist

- Wet cutting or extraction in use  
- Cutting position chosen carefully  
- Doors/windows avoided  
- Nearby surfaces protected  
- Area cleaned after cutting  

Ticking these five keeps most brick and block cutting jobs from becoming everyone else’s problem.
