## The problem

Cutting concrete indoors creates fine silica dust. You don’t see it at first. You taste it later. It lingers in rooms, travels down corridors, and settles in lungs that were never asked for the job.

Most indoor cutting goes wrong for three reasons:

- The tool has no dust control at all.
- The dust control exists but isn’t connected, fitted, or switched on.
- The extraction is underpowered, blocked, or shared between too many tools.

The result is the same: dust in the air, complaints from occupants, and a job that quietly becomes a health problem.

## What “good” looks like on an indoor cut

A workable indoor setup has three parts:

1. **The tool is set up for extraction**  
   Use a saw with a proper shroud or integrated dust port. If the blade is throwing dust straight into the room, you’re already losing.

2. **The extractor matches the task**  
   Small handheld saws need an M-class (or better) extractor with enough airflow to keep up. Domestic vacuums are not dust control. They clog, leak, and put fine dust straight back into the room.

3. **The space is controlled, not just the tool**  
   Indoors, dust control isn’t just about the cut. It’s about:
   - Closing doors
   - Sealing gaps where dust drifts into other rooms
   - Working in sections instead of chasing long cuts across open spaces

## Practical controls that actually help

These are the things that reduce dust in real rooms, not just on paper:

- **On-tool extraction**  
  Shroud + hose + M-class extractor. Simple, effective, and usually the best first move.

- **Local containment**  
  Temporary screens or zip walls keep dust where the work is happening instead of letting it migrate.

- **Wet cutting (only where appropriate)**  
  Works well for dust suppression, but creates slurry and slip risks. Indoors, wet cutting often trades dust for cleanup problems. Use with intention, not habit.

- **Work sequencing**  
  Do the dustiest cuts in one session. Clean thoroughly before moving on. Drifting dust is often a result of repeated small dusty tasks spread across the day.

## What to avoid

- **Dry cutting with no extraction**  
  This is the fastest way to fill a room with respirable dust.

- **Using a domestic vacuum**  
  They leak fine dust, clog quickly, and give a false sense of control.

- **Letting hoses kink or clog**  
  A blocked hose turns a good setup into a bad one instantly.

- **Assuming “quick cuts” don’t matter**  
  Five short cuts done badly can be worse than one longer cut done properly.

## PPE: the last line, not the first

Respiratory protection matters, but it shouldn’t be your main control. Masks help the person holding the tool. They do nothing for everyone else in the room.

If people nearby can smell dust, you’re relying too much on PPE and not enough on control at source.

## A simple indoor cutting setup

If you need a no-nonsense starting point:

- Handheld concrete saw with shroud
- M-class extractor with clean filter
- Short, well-supported hose run
- Temporary screen or sealed doorway
- Basic clean-down before moving to the next room

This isn’t perfect. It’s just good enough to stop most indoor jobs turning into a dust cloud.

## UK context

In the UK, employers are expected to control exposure to respirable crystalline silica. That doesn’t mean zero dust. It means you’re expected to use sensible, known controls rather than pretending a mask alone solves it.

If you’re cutting concrete indoors without extraction, you’re likely below what’s considered reasonable control on a modern site.

## Quick checklist

- Tool has a shroud
- Extractor is M-class and running
- Doors and openings controlled
- Hoses clear and filters clean
- Area cleaned before moving on

If you tick all five, you’re doing better than most indoor cutting setups.
